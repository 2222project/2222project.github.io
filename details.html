Details about our approach

1.5% per year

We plan to distribute around 1.5% of assets per year. Our simulations show that we can maximize cumulative distributions over the first 50 years by picking any distribution rate between 1.5% and 6%. But after the first 50 years, lower distribution rates do dramatically better.

1.5% is just a target. If the endowment grows more slowly than expected then we will reduce distributions to as low as 0.5%. And after we reach our goal we will increase distributions, to 6% or more. These adjustments help us meet our goals even if the market performs badly.

5% annual growth

Equities have historically grown at about 6.8% per year after inflation and including dividends [1]. Actual annual returns vary dramatically, but the distribution of annual returns has been stable over the past 150 years, therefore it's reasonable to expect that distribution to persist far into the future.

After subtracting 1.5% for annual distributions and another 0.1% for overhead, just over 5% per year remains. We reinvest that into the endowment, to drive growth. A 5% annual growth rate means assets and distributions should both double every 14 years or so.

Reaching everyone

100 million people

Currently in the US, adults over the age of 18 living in poverty make up about 10% of the population, or around 32 million people [1]. By the year 2222, based on US census projections we expect that the overall population will have tripled to about 1 billion people [2]. So we need to plan for 100 million people living in poverty in the future.

We focus on adults over 18 and on individuals rather than households, in order to avoid incentivizing any particular family structure.

$12,000 per person

The goal is to be able to pay adults a basic income of $12,000 per year. That's about the same as the US federal poverty level for individuals [1], slightly more than the poverty gap for households living in poverty [2], and roughly the average income of the bottom quintile of households [3].

For the first few decades we may distribute smaller amounts per person, down to as little as $100. This will allow us to reach more people, and study the impact of different sizes of transfers.

$1 per person to start

A $10 investment today will likely be able to support one person in the year 2222. To eliminate poverty in 10% of the population, we therefore need about $1 per person, or about $1 billion in seed capital. If we raise more then we can solve poverty sooner; if we raise less then it will take longer. Either way, the impact per $1 invested will be the same.

2% of the market

A $1 billion investment should grow enough over 200 years to enable distributions of $1.2 trillion per year, paid from an endowent with assets of $25 trillion. For comparison, $1.2 trillion is more than three times total charitable giving in the US [1], and $25 trillion is the current value of the entire US stock market. Fortunately the economy also grows. If GDP continues to grow at a relatively conservative 2% over inflation [2], we will own roughly 2% of the total stock market.
